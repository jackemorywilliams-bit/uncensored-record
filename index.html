Researcher's Journal

The Story Behind This Course

This course did not begin in a library. It began with a feeling: a creeping suspicion that the history being taught in Georgia classrooms was shaped by forces students could not see. Here is how that intuition became a research project.

üí°

Inspiration

The Question That Started Everything

The spark came from a simple classroom observation: students could recite the dates of World War II battles but had never heard of the 19-year U.S. occupation of Haiti. Why did some histories get told, and others simply vanish? That question became the foundation of this research.

"The antidote to feel-good history is not feel-bad history but honest and inclusive history." ‚Äî James W. Loewen, Lies My Teacher Told Me (1995)
üîç

'Aha' Moment

When the Data Confirmed the Feeling

Surveying 103 students at a Georgia high school, the numbers were striking. Only 22 of those students had ever been taught about the U.S. overthrow of Allende in Chile. The silence was not random. Events that implicated American power tended to disappear from the historical record entirely.

"The omission is not an accident. It is a curriculum choice, and that choice has consequences for how students understand democracy." ‚Äî Emory Williams, researcher
‚ö°

Unexpected Finding

Students Want More, Not Less

The assumption behind censorship laws is that students need protection from difficult history. The data said the opposite. 83% of the 103 students surveyed actively wanted a more comprehensive curriculum. The students most affected by these restrictions were, through their own survey responses, arguing against them.

"If the students most affected are calling for greater complexity, whose interests are these policies actually serving?" ‚Äî Emory Williams, researcher

The Larger Pattern

History Has Seen This Before

Digging into historical precedent revealed a sobering pattern. The Ming Dynasty, the Roman Empire, Nazi Germany: all enforced educational censorship, and all collapsed. The research shifted from a local study of Georgia into a much larger argument about the relationship between honest history and democratic stability.

"History is important. More than any other topic, it is about us. Whether one deems our present society wondrous or awful or both, history reveals how we got to this point." ‚Äî James W. Loewen, Lies My Teacher Told Me (1995)

Foreign Policy ¬∑ 1899‚Äì1902

The Philippine-American War: America's First Imperial Project

+

Following the Spanish-American War of 1898, Spain ceded the Philippines to the United States under the Treaty of Paris for $20 million. When it became clear that U.S. forces intended to impose colonial control rather than grant independence, Filipino nationalists under Emilio Aguinaldo launched a war of resistance. The conflict lasted three years and resulted in the deaths of over 4,200 American soldiers and at least 20,000 Filipino combatants. As many as 200,000 Filipino civilians died from violence, famine, and disease. Only 46 of the 103 students surveyed had ever encountered this history in school.

Source: U.S. Office of the Historian, history.state.gov

What Actually Happened

The war unfolded in two phases. The first was a conventional military campaign. The second, beginning in late 1899, saw Filipino forces shift to guerrilla tactics. The United States responded with brutal counterinsurgency methods, including the torture of prisoners and the killing of civilians in suspected insurgent villages. American commanders publicly acknowledged using water torture. U.S. forces burned villages and forced entire populations into "reconcentration zones." The Balangiga massacre of 1901, in which Filipino fighters killed American soldiers during church bells, prompted a U.S. general to order that anyone capable of carrying a weapon be killed, effectively approving mass civilian killing.

Why It Was Omitted

This war directly contradicts the self-image America has built around its foreign policy as a liberating, anti-colonial force. Teaching it would require students to reckon with the fact that the United States, at the very moment it was celebrating its democratic ideals abroad, was engaged in a violent colonial occupation. That tension is exactly what censorship works to prevent.

Key Topics in This Module

  • The Treaty of Paris (1898) and how the Philippines passed from Spain to U.S. control for $20 million
  • Emilio Aguinaldo and the Filipino Republic's declaration of independence
  • Two phases of the war: conventional combat and guerrilla resistance
  • American atrocities: the water cure, reconcentration zones, and the Balangiga aftermath
  • Civilian death toll of up to 200,000 and how official records minimized it
  • The gap between democratic rhetoric and colonial action as a recurring American pattern

Module Check

What was the immediate cause of the Philippine-American War breaking out in February 1899?

Correct. Just two days before the U.S. Senate ratified the Treaty of Paris, fighting broke out between American forces and Filipino nationalists led by Emilio Aguinaldo, who sought independence rather than a change in colonial rulers. The United States had purchased the Philippines from Spain without consulting the Filipino people.

Foreign Policy ¬∑ 1915‚Äì1934

The U.S. Occupation of Haiti: Democracy Suspended

+

Following the assassination of the Haitian president in July 1915, President Woodrow Wilson sent U.S. Marines into Haiti, beginning a 19-year military occupation. The United States took control of Haitian finances, forced the election of a pro-American president, dismantled the Haitian legislature, and in 1917 imposed a new constitution that allowed foreign land ownership, reversing a prohibition dating back to the Haitian Revolution. Haiti is the second-oldest republic in the Western Hemisphere and the world's first Black republic. Only 50 of the 103 students surveyed had ever heard of this occupation.

Source: U.S. Office of the Historian, history.state.gov

What Actually Happened

The occupation introduced the corv√©e labor system, requiring Haitian civilians to perform forced labor on road construction and public works projects under Marine enforcement. Haitian resistance, led by the Caco fighters, was suppressed militarily. The U.S. also established the Haitian Gendarmerie, a military force controlled by American officers. Between 1911 and 1915, seven Haitian presidents had been assassinated or overthrown, increasing U.S. anxieties about foreign influence. In actuality, the occupation protected U.S. financial assets in the country and prevented a possible German presence in the Caribbean.

Why It Was Omitted

The occupation exposes the racial logic underlying American imperialism. Haiti represented something that threatened the racial order the United States was built on: a Black nation that had defeated a European colonial power and governed itself. Teaching this occupation requires asking uncomfortable questions about who American democracy was designed to protect.

Key Topics in This Module

  • Haiti's revolutionary history as the world's first Black republic (1804) and the U.S. response to its existence
  • The 1914 removal of $500,000 from the Haitian National Bank to New York by U.S. Marines
  • The 1917 constitution, foreign land ownership, and the erasure of Haitian sovereignty
  • The corv√©e forced labor system enforced by U.S. Marines
  • Haitian resistance: the Caco Wars and the suppression of Charlemagne P√©ralte
  • Long-term consequences for Haitian governance and economic sovereignty

Module Check

What did the U.S.-imposed 1917 Haitian constitution change that was considered especially significant by Haitians?

Correct. Since the Haitian Revolution, foreign land ownership had been banned as a protection against the reintroduction of colonial control. The Wilson administration used a new constitution to reverse this, opening Haitian land to foreign investors. Many Haitians saw this as a direct attack on the meaning of their independence.

Cold War ¬∑ 1954

Operation PBSUCCESS: The CIA Overthrow of Guatemalan Democracy

+

In August 1953, the Operations Coordinating Board directed the CIA to assume primary responsibility for operations against the elected Guatemalan government of President Jacobo √Årbenz Guzm√°n. Operation PBSUCCESS, as it was officially designated, involved cutting off military aid to Guatemala, economic pressure, psychological warfare, and paramilitary action. On June 27, 1954, √Årbenz resigned. A CIA-backed military junta took power. What followed were decades of military dictatorship, genocide against Indigenous Mayan populations, and a civil war that killed an estimated 200,000 people. Only 44 of the 103 students surveyed had studied this intervention.

Source: U.S. Office of the Historian, Foreign Relations of the United States, 1952‚Äì1954, Guatemala, history.state.gov

What Actually Happened

√Årbenz's land reform program threatened the United Fruit Company, which held enormous landholdings in Guatemala and had significant connections to the Eisenhower administration. CIA Director Allen Dulles and Secretary of State John Foster Dulles both had prior ties to United Fruit. Declassified CIA documents confirm that the agency ran a clandestine radio station, funded opposition groups, and coordinated air operations, including strafing missions, to undermine the government. The Cold War framing of √Årbenz as a communist threat was used to justify what was fundamentally a corporate interest operation.

Why It Was Omitted

Guatemala makes visible something the standard curriculum works hard to obscure: that Cold War ideology was frequently used as cover for protecting corporate interests, not democratic ones. Teaching it honestly requires explaining that a banana company's lobbying directly resulted in the destruction of a democracy and the deaths of hundreds of thousands of people.

Key Topics in This Module

  • √Årbenz's land reform and the United Fruit Company's political influence in Guatemala and Washington
  • Allen Dulles (CIA) and John Foster Dulles (State Dept.) and their ties to United Fruit
  • Declassified PBSUCCESS documents: radio warfare, paramilitary operations, air strikes
  • The fall of √Årbenz on June 27, 1954, and the installation of a military junta
  • Decades of U.S.-backed military rule, the Guatemalan genocide, and Operation Condor
  • How corporate lobbying can shape the foreign policy of a democratic government

Cold War ¬∑ 1973

Chile and the Fall of Allende: When America Chose Dictatorship

+

On September 11, 1973, the Chilean military, with U.S. support, bombed the presidential palace and overthrew Salvador Allende, the world's first democratically elected Marxist head of state. Allende died in the final hours of the coup. On September 13, General Augusto Pinochet was named president. He dismantled Congress, outlawed left-wing political parties, and oversaw the torture and forced disappearance of thousands of political opponents. The coup ended 46 years of democratic rule in Chile. This was the least-taught event in the entire survey: only 22 of 103 students had ever encountered it.

Source: U.S. Office of the Historian, "The Allende Years and the Pinochet Coup," history.state.gov

What Actually Happened

The United States had a long history of covert action in Chile before the coup. The Nixon administration funded opposition candidates, ran anti-Allende propaganda campaigns, and actively discussed supporting a military coup as early as 1970. Declassified documents show the U.S. had considered a plan called Track II, a covert action meant to organize a coup before Allende could be inaugurated. A Senate committee convened in 1975 concluded the United States had carried out extensive covert operations in Chile during these years. Nixon's directive to make the Chilean economy "scream" was an instruction to deliberately destabilize a functioning democracy.

Why It Was Omitted

Chile is among the most direct examples in American history of the United States destroying the very thing it claimed to be defending. There is no honest version of this story that does not require students to sit with that contradiction, which is precisely why it tends to disappear from curricula.

Key Topics in This Module

  • Allende's 1970 election and the Nixon administration's immediate response: "make the economy scream"
  • Track I and Track II: covert U.S. plans to prevent Allende from taking office
  • The assassination of Chilean General Ren√© Schneider during a U.S.-connected kidnap plot in 1970
  • September 11, 1973: the bombing of La Moneda palace and the death of Allende
  • Pinochet's DINA secret police, torture, forced disappearances, and Operation Condor
  • The 1975 U.S. Senate investigation and what declassified documents confirmed

Module Check

How many years of continuous democratic rule in Chile did the 1973 coup bring to an end?

Correct. According to the U.S. Office of the Historian, the takeover of the government ended a 46-year history of democratic rule in Chile. This fact alone makes the coup one of the most significant Cold War events for understanding American foreign policy, and one of the most conspicuously absent from standard U.S. history curricula.

Domestic History ¬∑ 1921

The Tulsa Race Massacre: The Destruction of Black Wall Street

+

On May 31 and June 1, 1921, a white mob attacked Tulsa's Greenwood District, one of the wealthiest Black communities in the United States, known as "Black Wall Street." In the span of 18 hours, 35 city blocks were burned to the ground. 1,256 homes were destroyed. More than 800 people were admitted to hospitals. Credible estimates of deaths range from 100 to 300, though mass graves found near Oaklawn Cemetery suggest the number may be higher. The Oklahoma Bureau of Vital Statistics officially recorded 36 dead. About 10,000 Black residents were left homeless. The event was then largely omitted from history books until the late 1990s.

Sources: National Endowment for the Humanities; Tulsa Historical Society; Encyclopaedia Britannica

What Actually Happened

City officials selected men, all of them white and some of them active participants in the violence, and deputized them as agents of the city government. Public officials provided firearms and ammunition to these individuals. The Oklahoma National Guard participated in the mass arrests of Black residents, removing them to holding centers at Convention Hall and the Fairgrounds, where some were held for up to eight days. Entering the Greenwood district, people stole, damaged, or destroyed personal property. 1,256 homes were deliberately burned. Not one criminal act committed during the massacre was ever prosecuted by any level of government. The Oklahoma Commission to Study the Tulsa Race Riot of 1921, authorized in 1997 and reporting in 2001, recommended reparations. No legislative action was ever taken.

Why It Was Omitted

The Tulsa massacre was not merely overlooked. Police records disappeared. Newspaper accounts were minimal. The state did not formally acknowledge the events until 2001. This was a sustained institutional effort to erase evidence of state-sanctioned racial violence from the historical record, not an accidental omission.

Key Topics in This Module

  • Greenwood's history and why it became the wealthiest Black community in the country
  • The Dick Rowland incident and the Tulsa Tribune's inflammatory reporting on May 31
  • The role of city officials in deputizing white mob members and providing them weapons
  • National Guard actions: mass arrest of Black residents while white attackers went free
  • The 75-year suppression of this history and the 2001 Oklahoma Commission findings
  • The ongoing search for mass graves and the failure to pay reparations to survivors

Module Check

What did the Oklahoma National Guard do during and after the Tulsa Race Massacre that illustrated official complicity in the violence?

Correct. According to the Tulsa Historical Society and the 2001 Oklahoma Commission report, the National Guard participated in the mass arrest of approximately 6,000 Black residents, who were held in holding centers for up to eight days. Not one criminal act committed during the massacre was ever prosecuted by any level of government.

Georgia and the Present ¬∑ 2022 to Today

The New Censorship: Georgia's Curriculum Laws and Their Cost

+

In 2022, Georgia passed three laws that have fundamentally altered history education across the state: the Divisive Concepts Law, the Parents' Bill of Rights, and the Harmful to Minors Law. Each restricts in different ways what teachers can teach and what students can learn about race, identity, and systemic injustice. This final module brings the historical pattern documented in the previous five modules into the present day.

What the Laws Actually Say

The Divisive Concepts Law bans instruction on topics framed as creating "division" around race, a deliberately vague standard that allows enforcement through fear as much as formal rule. The Parents' Bill of Rights grants parents the ability to remove books or entire curriculum units. The Harmful to Minors Law enables broad content removal on subjective grounds, language that has been directed increasingly at texts addressing gender, sexuality, or racial injustice in the classroom.

What the Original Survey Found

In original research conducted with 103 students at Pope High School in Marietta, Georgia, 76% reported that censorship laws had significantly or somewhat altered their education. 87% agreed that comprehensive historical literacy was essential for civic participation. And 83% said they wanted a broader curriculum. The students most affected by these laws expressed the clearest desire to learn more, not less.

The Historical Pattern

As scholar James Loewen's research demonstrates, students taught a mythologized version of their country's history tend toward disengagement rather than active citizenship. The historical record is consistent: the Ming Dynasty's censorship stifled intellectual inquiry and contributed to stagnation; Nazi Germany's rewriting of history produced a population that absorbed state propaganda as unchallengeable fact. The pattern does not change when the country is the United States.

Key Topics in This Module

  • The text and practical implications of Georgia's three 2022 censorship laws
  • Survey findings from 103 Georgia students on curriculum, censorship, and civic identity (Emory Williams, 2024)
  • James Loewen's research on the civic effects of incomplete historical education
  • Historical parallels: Ming Dynasty, Roman Empire, Nazi Germany
  • The gap between what policymakers say these laws protect and what students actually experience
  • What students, teachers, and citizens can do in response

Module Check

Which of Georgia's three 2022 censorship laws granted parents the ability to have specific books or curriculum units removed from their child's school?

Correct. The Parents' Bill of Rights specifically granted parents veto power over curriculum materials, including the ability to remove entire units or ban specific books from their child's education. The Divisive Concepts Law restricts how certain topics can be framed, while the Harmful to Minors Law enables broader content removal on subjective grounds.
